Use the previous exercise to argue that

\begin{equation*} ax \equiv b \Mod{m} \end{equation*}

has a unique solution (up to congruence class) if \(\gcd(a,m) = 1\text{.}\)

Does the converse hold? That is, if we know that

\begin{equation*} ax \equiv b \Mod{m} \end{equation*}

has a unique solution, can we conclude that \(\gcd(a,m) = 1\text{?}\)
